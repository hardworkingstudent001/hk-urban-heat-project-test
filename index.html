The Paradox of a "Green City"

Hong Kong proudly claims that 65% of its land is covered by woodland and shrubland. Yet its citizens are among the most heat-stressed in Asia. How can a "green city" be so hot?

The answer lies in a cruel irony of urban planning: while the mountains and country parks remain pristine and cool, 7.5 million people are squeezed into just 7% of the land—creating concrete canyons that trap heat long after the sun sets.

Using real-time data from the Hong Kong Observatory, population census records, and land utilization surveys, we've uncovered the hidden geography of Hong Kong's Urban Heat Islands.

01

Heat Intensity by District

The Intensity Score reveals which districts bear the highest temperature burden— and how extreme conditions can become.

📊 Key Findings

Hottest Districts

Yuen Long and Southern/Yau Tsim Mong record the highest intensity scores, with temperatures reaching 37-38°C

Coolest Districts

North District and Tuen Mun show the lowest scores, typically staying at 33-34°C

What does Intensity Score mean?

The Intensity Score measures the maximum heat burden experienced by residents in each district. A higher score indicates that people in that area are exposed to more extreme temperatures during peak heat hours.

02

Urban Heat Delta

The difference between day and night temperatures reveals the true "heat trap" effect of concrete jungles.

🌡️ Understanding the Delta

High Delta More concrete → Smaller day/night difference → Stronger heat island effect
Low Delta More greenery → Larger day/night difference → Natural cooling at night
Highest Urban Heat Effect

Sai Kung and Tsuen Wan show the highest delta values—nights stay almost as hot as days.

Best Natural Cooling

North District and Yuen Long (Delta: -2.00) show the largest temperature drops at night.

🔍 Case Study: Yuen Long Paradox

Yuen Long presents a fascinating paradox: it has the lowest Urban Heat Delta (-2.00), meaning large day-night temperature swings. Yet its daytime Intensity Score reaches 37-38°C—among the highest in Hong Kong.

Conclusion: While Yuen Long cools down significantly at night, its residents still suffer extreme heat during the day. This suggests that nighttime relief doesn't offset daytime heat stress.

05

Insights & Recommendations

1

The Heat Island is a Planning Problem

Our data reveals that Hong Kong's Urban Heat Island effect is not merely a weather phenomenon—it's a direct consequence of how we've distributed 7.5 million people across just 7% of available land.

Evidence: Districts with highest concrete coverage (Yau Tsim Mong, Kwun Tong) consistently show the smallest day-night temperature differences.
2

Nighttime Heat is the Hidden Killer

While daytime temperatures grab headlines, our Urban Heat Delta analysis shows that the inability to cool down at night may pose greater health risks, especially for the elderly.

Evidence: Sai Kung and Tsuen Wan show the highest delta values—their nights remain nearly as hot as their days.
3

Density Alone Doesn't Determine Heat

Population density correlates with heat stress, but isn't the only factor. Urban design, ventilation corridors, and green coverage can mitigate effects even in dense areas.

4

Vulnerable Populations Need Targeted Relief

Over 2 million residents live in high-risk heat zones. These areas should be prioritized for cooling centers, green infrastructure, and heat warning systems.

Recommendations for Future Development

🌳

Cooling Corridors

Future "nano-flat" developments must integrate airflow paths and green corridors to prevent heat accumulation.

🏗️

Building Materials

Mandate heat-reflective surfaces and green roofs in new constructions, especially in already-stressed districts.

📱

Early Warning Systems

Implement district-level heat alerts using real-time HKO data to protect vulnerable populations.

🗺️

Zoning Reform

Reconsider development density caps in high-stress districts and incentivize decentralization to cooler areas.

Analysis Methods

  • Intensity Score: Calculated from maximum hourly temperature readings across each district over the study period.
  • Urban Heat Delta: Derived from the difference between average daytime (10:00-18:00) and nighttime (22:00-06:00) temperatures.
  • High-Risk Population: Defined as residents in areas with both above-median temperature and above-median density.
